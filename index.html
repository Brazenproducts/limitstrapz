Suspension Limit Straps — Complete Buyer's Guide 2026

Limit straps are one of the most important and least glamorous suspension components on an off-road build. They prevent your shocks and CV axles from over-extending under full droop — which means they're actively preventing expensive damage every time your suspension cycles to full travel. Getting the right strap length and construction quality matters.

Why Limit Straps Matter

When a wheel drops into a hole or off a ledge, suspension extends to full droop. Without a limit strap, a CV axle can over-extend past its maximum angle, binding and snapping under load. Shocks can also reach the end of their travel and be damaged by hydraulic shock. A properly sized limit strap takes the mechanical load of full droop before any of these failures can occur.

Choosing the Right Length

Limit strap length is measured from mounting point to mounting point at full droop. The strap should engage just before — or right at — the point where CV axle angle becomes critical. Most aftermarket suspension manufacturers publish recommended strap lengths for their kits. If you're unsure, err slightly shorter rather than longer — a strap that engages too early just limits travel, a strap that's too long defeats the purpose.

Construction: Polyester vs. Nylon vs. Webbing

High-quality limit straps use woven polyester or nylon webbing with reinforced metal end loops. Bartact's limit straps use MIL-SPEC webbing rated well beyond any realistic vehicle load. Camburg Engineering straps use aircraft-quality hardware. Avoid Chinese off-brand straps with unknown webbing ratings — the failure mode is silent until it's not, usually at the worst possible moment on the trail.

Installation: Key Points

Mount limit straps to solid structural points — control arm brackets, frame tabs, or dedicated mount points. Never mount to body panels or non-structural brackets. Torque all hardware to spec. After install, cycle the suspension through full travel to verify strap length and confirm there's no binding or unexpected interference with other components.

Matching Straps to Your Suspension System

If you're running a lift kit, the correct strap length changes. A 4-inch lift significantly changes droop geometry compared to stock. Most suspension kit manufacturers include correctly sized limit straps in the kit — using those as a starting point is safer than guessing. For custom builds, measure the actual droop travel from ride height to full droop and size accordingly.

Frequently Asked Questions

What do suspension limit straps actually do?

Limit straps prevent suspension from over-extending at full droop — which protects CV axles from binding at extreme angles and prevents shocks from reaching hydraulic damage limits. They take the mechanical load of full droop before any drivetrain or suspension component reaches its damage threshold. On lifted vehicles that see real off-road use, they're essential.

How do I know what length limit straps I need?

Measure from your mounting points at ride height to the same points at full droop — that measurement gives you the maximum travel. The strap should engage just before the CV axle reaches its critical angle, which is typically 25-30 degrees depending on the joint. Most aftermarket suspension kit manufacturers publish recommended strap lengths for their specific kits.

Can I run my Jeep without limit straps?

You can, but without limit straps the CV axles can over-extend and break under load if the wheel drops into a hole. For street-only driving with little suspension travel, the risk is low. For anything approaching real off-road use — especially with a lift — limit straps are protection insurance for expensive drivetrain components.

Are expensive limit straps worth the money?

Yes. Cheap limit straps with cast zinc hardware and unrated webbing can fail silently — the webbing begins to tear microscopically before visible failure. MIL-SPEC or aircraft-quality webbing and forged/machined hardware have defined failure loads. Bartact, Camburg, and Fox use rated materials. Budget brands typically don't publish ratings because the ratings aren't good.

How do I install suspension limit straps?

Mount to solid structural attachment points only — control arm brackets, frame tabs, or purpose-built mount points. Never use body panels or flimsy brackets. After install, cycle the suspension through full travel and verify the strap engages at the right point without creating binding or hitting other components. Torque all hardware to specification.

Do I need limit straps if my lift kit included them?

Most quality lift kits from Teraflex, Rough Country, and Skyjacker include limit straps sized for their kit's travel. If your kit included them and they're properly installed, you're covered for that lift height. If you subsequently change the suspension configuration — add a body lift, swap shocks, or change control arms — recheck strap length.

What's the difference between limit straps and bump stops?

Bump stops limit suspension compression (wheel travel upward) — they're rubber buffers that prevent the suspension from bottoming out hard. Limit straps limit extension (wheel travel downward, droop). Both are needed for complete suspension protection on lifted vehicles. They work on opposite ends of the travel range.

How long do quality limit straps last?

MIL-SPEC polyester and nylon webbing has essentially indefinite life under normal use conditions. UV exposure is the main degradation factor — check straps annually for fraying, UV bleaching, or visible damage. Properly installed limit straps on a well-maintained vehicle will outlast the vehicle itself in most cases.
